Joaquin Phoenix Movies, Endorsements, and Books

Movies

Joaquin started his acting career young with movies like Kids Don’t Tell, Spacecamp, Parenthood, and To Die For. His initial movies were most independently produced movies and didn’t garner much attention and money.

Gladiator, released in 2000, became the first blockbuster movie of Joaquin Phoenix. The movie starred Russel Crowe as the protagonist, and Joaquin played Commodus- antagonist of the movie.

Gladiator collected over $460 million at the box office, boosting Joaquins Career and making him a favorite in Hollywood.

Even though starring in various critically successful movies like Signs, Walk The Line, We Own the Night & I’m Still here, his movies couldn’t collect much at the box office.

Joaquin had to wait for another 19 years to start in the record-shattering movie Joker, released in 2019.

Director of the movie Todd Philips and Joaquin Phoenix received grave acclaim for the movie, especially Joaquin.

The movie earned around 1 billion in the box office and went on to win Oscars for two categories, best original score, and best actor.

3 Facts about Joaquin Phoenix

  • Joaquin had changed his name to “Leaf” in his childhood, and he appeared in several movies with this credited name. The reason was he wanted to have a name close to nature, just like his siblings with names like Rain, River, and Summer, and he thought the name was hard to pronounce. But at the age of 15, he officially adapted his birth name “Joaquin” after filming for the movie “Walking the Dog” and is known as that same since.
  • His brother River Phoenix was also a great actor but unfortunately lost his life at the young age of 23 years. These two brothers hold a distinctive record of being nominated for Academy Awards in Acting.
  •  Joaquin retired from acting in 2008 to pursue a career in hip hop. He appeared in several shows citing his retirement and putting up a shady character with a beard. But it was later revelated that all the fuzz was for the mockumentary “I’m Still Here,” and the act was just a prank.
